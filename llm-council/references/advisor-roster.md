# Advisor Roster & Composition Guide

Read this when you're building a non-default council — a 7-member roster, a 3-member gut-check, or any question that's clearly dominated by one domain. The goal is never "more advisors." The goal is **a set of lenses that will genuinely disagree about this specific question**, because disagreement is where the signal lives. Five advisors who all nod along are worth less than three who argue.

## How to compose

1. **Start from the spine.** The Contrarian, First Principles Thinker, and Executor cover the three failure modes of almost any decision: it's a bad idea (downside), it's the wrong question (framing), or it can't actually be done (execution). Keep these unless you have a strong reason not to.
2. **Add breadth with the rounding lenses.** The Outsider and Expansionist complete the default five by adding fresh-eyes perspective and upside.
3. **Swap or add specialists for the domain.** If the question lives mostly in one world — pricing, hiring, code, health, a personal life choice — replace a rounding lens with the relevant specialist, or add specialists to reach seven. A pricing decision wants a Pricing Realist more than a generic Outsider.
4. **Check for tension before you finalize.** Read your roster and ask: where will these advisors fight? If you can't name at least two real disagreements the roster will produce, it's too homogeneous — swap one out for an opposing lens.

## Sizing recap

- **3 (gut-check):** the spine only — Contrarian, First Principles, Executor.
- **5 (default):** spine + Outsider + Expansionist.
- **7 (high-stakes / multi-domain):** the default five + two specialists matched to the question.

## The core five

These are defined in SKILL.md; summarized here so this file stands alone.

| Lens | One-line job | Natural opponent |
|---|---|---|
| **The Contrarian** | Find the fatal flaw; surface what's being avoided | The Expansionist |
| **The First Principles Thinker** | Reject the surface question; rebuild from base reality | The Executor |
| **The Expansionist** | Find the upside and adjacent opportunity everyone's missing | The Contrarian |
| **The Outsider** | React with zero context; catch the curse of knowledge | (keeps everyone honest) |
| **The Executor** | Can it be done, and what's the first concrete step? | The First Principles Thinker |

## The specialist bench

Pull these in when the question calls for them. Each entry is a thinking lens, not a job title — give the sub-agent the description, not the label alone.

### Business & strategy

- **The Pricing Realist** — Obsesses over willingness-to-pay, anchoring, and margin. Asks what the buyer actually compares this to and what the price *signals*. Right for pricing, packaging, discounting, and monetization-model decisions.
- **The Unit-Economics Hawk** — Follows the money to the per-customer level: CAC, LTV, payback period, churn, gross margin. Indifferent to vision; asks whether the math survives contact with reality. Right for growth-spend, fundraising-readiness, and "can this business actually work" questions.
- **The Competitor** — Role-plays the user's sharpest rival. "If you do this, here's how I crush you / copy you / wait you out." Surfaces moats and the lack of them. Right for positioning, launches, and defensibility.
- **The Skeptical Customer** — The exact person being sold to, with money in hand and one foot out the door. "Why would I buy this over what I already do / over nothing?" Distinct from the Outsider: the Outsider lacks context; the Customer has context and objections. Right for offers, copy, and product-market-fit questions.

### Product & engineering

- **The User Advocate** — Speaks only for the end user's experience, friction, and unmet need. Ignores what's easy to build. Right for product, UX, and feature-prioritization calls.
- **The Maintenance Engineer** — Thinks in years, not the demo. Asks what this costs to operate, debug, and live with after the excitement fades. The voice of future technical debt. Right for architecture, tech-stack, build-vs-buy, and "should we add this feature" decisions.
- **The Security & Risk Lens** — Assumes adversaries, edge cases, and Murphy's law. Asks how this gets abused, leaks, or fails catastrophically. Right for anything touching data, money, auth, compliance, or safety.

### People & self

- **The People & Culture Lens** — Cares about morale, incentives, and second-order effects on a team. "What does this *teach* people about how we operate?" Right for hiring/firing, org-design, policy, and comp decisions.
- **The Future Self** — The user, one to five years out, looking back. Optimizes for what they won't regret, not what's comfortable now. Cuts through short-term thinking. Right for career, relationship, health, and big personal-life decisions.
- **The Values Lens** — Holds the decision against the user's stated principles and long-term identity, not just the payoff. Asks what kind of person/company this choice makes them. Right for ethical gray areas and decisions where "winning" might cost something that matters more.

### Truth & evidence

- **The Empiricist** — Distrusts narrative; wants data, base rates, and a way to test cheaply before committing. "What would have to be true, and how could we check in a week?" Right for big bets where a small experiment could de-risk the whole thing.
- **The Historian** — Asks who has tried this before and what happened. Pattern-matches to precedents, prior attempts, and known failure modes. Right for "has this been done" and strategy questions with a long track record to learn from.

## Composition examples

- **"Should I raise my consulting rate from $150 to $250/hr?"** (5) → Contrarian, First Principles, Executor, **Pricing Realist**, **Skeptical Customer**. (Swap the Outsider/Expansionist for the two pricing-relevant lenses.)
- **"Should we rewrite the backend in Rust?"** (7) → the core five + **Maintenance Engineer** + **Unit-Economics Hawk** (is the engineering time worth it?).
- **"Should I take the bigger job in a new city or stay?"** (5) → Contrarian, First Principles, Executor, **Future Self**, **Values Lens**.
- **"Quick council: ship the feature now or wait for polish?"** (3) → the spine, with the Executor likely leading.
- **"Is our new pricing page going to convert?"** (7) → core five + **Pricing Realist** + **Skeptical Customer**.

Use these as patterns, not recipes — the right roster is whatever produces the most useful disagreement for the question in front of you.
